Managing Exam Anxiety
🧠
Reframe your anxiety as excitement
Research shows telling yourself "I am excited" outperforms "I am calm." Both are high-arousal states — excitement is just positively labelled anxiety.
📝
Write your worries down before the exam
10 minutes of expressive writing the morning of your exam frees up working memory that worry normally consumes. Students who do this score measurably higher.
👁️
Use the "5-4-3-2-1" grounding technique
Name 5 things you can see, 4 you can touch, 3 you can hear, 2 you can smell, 1 you can taste. This interrupts the anxiety loop by engaging all your senses.
⏸️
Pause before answering hard questions
When you hit a difficult question, take 3 slow breaths before attempting it. Skip it and return later — coming back with fresh eyes improves accuracy by 15–20%.
😴
Sleep & Recovery
🕙
Sleep consolidates memory
What you study the day before is transferred from short-term to long-term memory during sleep. Pulling an all-nighter erases this process entirely.
📵
No screens 1 hour before bed
Blue light suppresses melatonin production by up to 50%, delaying sleep onset by an average of 90 minutes. Read a physical book or revise notes on paper instead.
⏰
Keep a consistent sleep schedule
Going to bed and waking at the same time — even on weekends — is more impactful than total hours slept. Your brain performs best when its internal clock is predictable.
🎯
Focus & Concentration
🍅
Use the Pomodoro technique
Study for 25 minutes, then rest for 5. After four rounds, take a 20-minute break. This matches your brain's natural ultradian rhythm and prevents mental fatigue.
🔕
Single-task, don't multi-task
Switching between tasks costs 23 minutes of lost focus each time. Close social media, put your phone face-down, and commit fully to one subject at a time.
🌿
Study near natural light when possible
Natural light increases serotonin which improves mood and focus. Students studying near windows show 15% better performance than those in artificial light only.
